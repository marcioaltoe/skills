# Persuasion playbook

The techniques that make a business document land. Apply them in this order: structure first, translation second, persuasion third, voice last.

## Structure: answer first

- **Pyramid principle.** Conclusion first, then the grouped arguments, then the supporting detail. The reader who stops after one paragraph still leaves with the decision and the reason.
- **Three-line executive summary.** Every document that asks for something opens with: (1) the recommendation or outcome in one sentence, (2) why it matters, with the strongest number, (3) what is needed from the reader and by when. If those three lines work, the document works.
- **Headline like a newspaper, not a label.** "Approving the new export cuts month-end closing by two days" — not "Export feature proposal". The first five words carry the outcome.
- **Scannable always.** Short paragraphs (four sentences max), informative subheadings, tables for comparisons, bold only on the few numbers that decide. A director should get the whole argument in a 30-second scan.
- **The pub test.** If the core message cannot be explained to a friend in 30 seconds, the thinking is not done. Clarify the thinking, then write.

## Audience: the CRG model

Every business reader weighs three things — Cost, Risk, Growth — but each department leads with a different one. Same facts, different lead:

| Reader                              | Leads with | Frame the same project as                                                      |
| ----------------------------------- | ---------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Finance, accounting, procurement    | **Cost**   | "Saves R$ X per month; pays for itself in N months"                            |
| Operations, support, compliance, HR | **Risk**   | "Eliminates the failure that caused N hours of downtime; keeps us audit-ready" |
| Leadership, sales, marketing        | **Growth** | "Unlocks the enterprise segment; no competitor offers this yet"                |

Pick the dominant reader and let their letter lead. The other two still appear — one document, one weighting. When the audience is the whole company, lead with the change in daily life ("what changes for you") and put cost/risk/growth in a short section each.

## Translation: capability → outcome

The reader does not need to understand the system; they need to know what changes for the company. Start from impact, work backwards.

| Technical fact                         | Business translation                                                          |
| -------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Refactored the authentication module" | "Login problems that generated ~N support tickets a month are gone"           |
| "Migrated the database to version 16"  | "The system now handles our growth for the next N years without slowing down" |
| "Reduced p95 latency to 200ms"         | "Screens respond instantly even on the busiest day of the month"              |
| "Paid down technical debt in billing"  | "Changes to billing rules now take days instead of weeks, with fewer errors"  |
| "Added automated test coverage"        | "New releases stopped breaking what already worked"                           |
| "Deployed CI/CD pipeline"              | "Fixes reach you in hours, not in the next monthly release"                   |

Rules:

- **Feature → benefit, every time.** "The platform has an automated workflow engine" says nothing; "you stop doing 4 hours of manual work per week" says everything.
- **Jargon gets translated or cut.** If a term must stay (audit, contract, compliance), define it in one plain sentence on first use — "API (the channel through which two systems exchange data automatically)".
- **Beware of analogies.** "It's like renovating a house's foundation" sounds helpful but sets false expectations. Prefer the real consequence over the metaphor; use an analogy only when it maps honestly.
- **Invisible work needs visible stakes.** Refactors and infrastructure have no screenshot — translate them as risk removed and speed gained, and quantify what doing nothing costs.

## Persuasion: behavioral science that is fair to use

- **Loss aversion — quantify the cost of inaction.** People feel a loss roughly twice as strongly as an equal gain. "We currently lose ~N hours per month to this manual process" moves more than "we will save N hours". Every proposal names what staying still costs — with real numbers only.
- **Concrete numbers beat quantifiers.** "87% of tickets resolved on first contact" persuades; "most tickets" could mean anything. Specific numbers signal that something was actually measured. Never round into fiction.
- **Social proof, internal edition.** "The finance team already uses this flow and closed the month two days early" lowers perceived risk more than any promise. Pilots, early adopters, and quotes from real colleagues are the strongest proof an internal document can carry.
- **Authority through evidence, not titles.** Cite the measurement, the audit, the benchmark, the incident report. A claim with a source reads as fact; a claim without one reads as opinion.
- **First sentence about the reader.** We remember what relates to us. Open with the reader's situation ("If you close the month in spreadsheets, this changes your week"), not with the team or the system.
- **Limit choices.** Present at most two or three options, recommend one, and say why. A menu of five options produces no decision.
- **One clear action.** End with a single next step: what, who, by when. "Approve the pilot by Friday so the rollout starts Monday."
- **Confident language.** "This eliminates the risk" — not "we believe this could potentially help mitigate". Hedging reads as doubt; if the evidence does not support confidence, fix the evidence, not the adverbs.
- **Front-load bad news.** Costs, limitations, and risks go early, each paired with its mitigation. Readers forgive problems; they do not forgive surprises — and a document that names its own weaknesses earns trust for everything else it claims.
- **The ethical line.** All of the above only with true facts. No false urgency, no invented social proof, no inflated risk. Persuasion helps the reader decide well; manipulation helps the writer win. The first builds a reputation, the second spends it.

## Storytelling: make it memorable

Facts embedded in a story are dramatically more memorable than facts in a list. Use these moves:

- **The reader is the hero.** The team is the guide, the software is the tool. "Your team closes the month in two days" — never "our brilliant solution". Documents that make the author the hero get admired; documents that make the reader the hero get approved.
- **Find the five-second moment.** Every story turns on one moment of change: the support agent who answers in seconds instead of minutes, the invoice that goes out right the first time. Build the narrative around that single concrete scene; everything else is context for it.
- **Start in the middle of the action.** "On the last business day of March, the system processed 40,000 invoices without a single error" beats any institutional opening.
- **Problems beat successes.** "Everything went well" is forgettable. "Month-end closing failed twice last year — here is what we changed so it never happens again" earns attention and trust.
- **Story spine** for announcements and explainers: _Every month, [who] had to [pain]. That cost [real number]. So we built [change, in business terms]. Now [new reality]. Which means [what the reader can do now]._
- **Hook, message, celebration.** Open with tension or a surprising number, deliver the change in the middle, close with the concrete win and the next step — never with "the future looks bright".
- **Coin the phrase.** Give the document one repeatable line — "close the month in two days", "fixes in hours, not months". If a director quotes it in the next meeting, the document did its job. One memorable phrase; a document full of slogans is marketing, not communication.
- **Paint the after-state.** One short paragraph of what daily work looks like once this is approved — specific enough that readers can see themselves in it.

## Voice: professional without stiffness

- Active voice; name who does what. "The finance team approves; we deploy" — not "it will be deployed upon approval".
- Plain words: "use" not "utilize", "start" not "initiate", "help" not "facilitate". Rich language means precise and vivid, not ornate.
- Vary the rhythm. A short sentence lands a point. A longer one carries the explanation that follows it. Uniform sentences anesthetize.
- Positive form: say what something is and does, not what it is not.
- Warm but direct. Write like a sharp colleague explaining something they care about — not a press release, not a legal notice, not a chatbot.

## Banned patterns

These mark a document as machine-generated filler or marketing fluff. Scan and rewrite every hit — the same rules apply in every output language (Portuguese equivalents in parentheses where they differ).

- **Inflated vocabulary:** leverage (alavancar), seamless, robust (robusto), cutting-edge, revolutionary (revolucionário), game-changer, crucial, pivotal, comprehensive, delve, streamline, empower (empoderar), unlock the potential (destravar o potencial), synergy (sinergia), state-of-the-art.
- **Filler:** "in order to" → "to"; "due to the fact that" → "because"; "it is important to note that" → delete ("é importante notar que" → delete); "at this point in time" → "now".
- **Hedging:** "we believe it could potentially help" → state it or cut it ("acreditamos que possivelmente poderia" → afirme ou corte).
- **Minimizers:** "simply", "easily", "just" ("simplesmente", "facilmente", "basta") — if it were easy, the document would not be needed.
- **Em-dash overuse:** machine writing leans on em dashes (—, travessões) for punchy rhythm. Budget: at most 2 em dashes in the whole document, never two in one sentence. Rewrite the rest with a comma, a period, or parentheses.
- **Superficial -ing tails:** "...ensuring efficiency", "...garantindo a eficiência" — cut the tail or replace it with a fact.
- **Negative parallelism:** "It's not just X, it's Y" ("não é apenas X, é Y").
- **Rule-of-three padding:** "fast, flexible, and powerful" — use the one adjective that is true and provable.
- **Decoration:** emojis, exclamation points, Title Case Headings.
- **Bold scatter and label-bullets:** bold at most 3 phrases in the whole document — the few numbers or dates that decide. Never write bullets shaped "**Label:** prose" ("**Cost:** ...", "**Risco:** ..."); turn each into a plain sentence ("The implementation costs R$ 24,000, paid once."), or use a table when the content is genuinely tabular.
- **Vague attribution:** "studies show", "users want", "estudos mostram" — name the study, the metric, the team, or cut the claim.
- **Sycophancy and chat artifacts:** "I hope this helps", "let me know", "Certainly!".
- **Generic endings:** "The future looks bright", "Exciting times ahead", "Contamos com todos!" — end with the one concrete next step.
